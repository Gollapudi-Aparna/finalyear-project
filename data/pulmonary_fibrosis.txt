Pulmonary Fibrosis

Overview Pulmonary fibrosis is a lung disease that occurs when lung
tissue becomes damaged and scarred. The thickened and stiff tissue makes
it harder for the lungs to function properly. The condition typically
worsens over time. Some people remain stable for long periods, while
others experience faster progression.

In some cases, the cause cannot be identified. When no cause is found,
the condition is called idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF).

Lung damage caused by pulmonary fibrosis cannot be reversed. However,
medications and therapies may help slow progression, relieve symptoms
and improve quality of life. In advanced cases, a lung transplant may be
considered.

Symptoms - Shortness of breath - Dry cough - Extreme tiredness
(fatigue) - Unintended weight loss - Aching muscles and joints -
Clubbing (widening and rounding of fingertips or toes)

Acute Exacerbation Symptoms, especially shortness of breath, may
suddenly worsen over days or weeks. This is called an acute exacerbation
and can be life-threatening. Immediate medical attention is required.

When to See a Doctor Contact a healthcare provider if you experience
persistent shortness of breath or dry cough. Seek urgent care if
symptoms worsen suddenly.

Causes Pulmonary fibrosis develops due to scarring and thickening of
tissue around the air sacs (alveoli). This makes it harder for oxygen to
pass into the bloodstream.

Possible Causes

Environmental and Occupational Exposure - Silica dust - Asbestos
fibers - Hard metal dust - Wood, coal and grain dust - Mold - Bird or
animal droppings

Radiation Therapy Radiation treatment to the chest for cancer (such as
lung or breast cancer) may cause lung damage months or years later.

Medications Certain medicines can damage lung tissue: - Chemotherapy
drugs (methotrexate, bleomycin, cyclophosphamide) - Heart medicines
(amiodarone) - Some antibiotics (nitrofurantoin, ethambutol) -
Anti-inflammatory medicines (rituximab, sulfasalazine)

Medical Conditions - Dermatomyositis - Lupus - Mixed connective tissue
disease - Pneumonia - Polymyositis - Rheumatoid arthritis -
Sarcoidosis - Scleroderma

Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF) When no cause is identified, the
condition is termed idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis. Risk factors such as
smoking or air pollution may contribute. Gastroesophageal reflux disease
(GERD) may also be associated with IPF.

Risk Factors - Smoking (current or former) - Certain occupations
(mining, farming, construction) - Exposure to lung-damaging pollutants -
Cancer treatments involving chest radiation or chemotherapy - Genetic
predisposition - Middle-aged or older adults (more common in this group)

Complications - Pulmonary hypertension (high blood pressure in lung
arteries) - Right-sided heart failure - Respiratory failure - Increased
risk of lung cancer - Blood clots in the lungs - Collapsed lung
(pneumothorax) - Lung infections

Prognosis Pulmonary fibrosis is typically progressive. Disease
progression varies between individuals. Early diagnosis and appropriate
management may help slow disease progression and improve quality of
life.
