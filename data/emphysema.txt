Emphysema

Overview Emphysema is a long-term lung disease that causes shortness of
breath. It damages the thin walls of the air sacs in the lungs called
alveoli. In healthy lungs, alveoli stretch and fill with air during
inhalation and recoil during exhalation. In emphysema, damaged alveoli
make it difficult to move air out of the lungs, preventing fresh
oxygen-rich air from entering.

Emphysema is one of the two main types of chronic obstructive pulmonary
disease (COPD). The other type is chronic bronchitis. Both conditions
often occur together.

Smoking is the leading cause of emphysema. The damage cannot be
reversed, but treatment can relieve symptoms and slow disease
progression.

Symptoms Symptoms often develop gradually and may include:

-   Shortness of breath (especially during physical activity)
-   Wheezing (whistling or squeaking sound when breathing out)
-   Persistent cough
-   Chest tightness or heaviness
-   Fatigue
-   Weight loss (in advanced stages)
-   Ankle swelling (in later stages)

In advanced cases, breathing difficulty may occur even at rest.

Exacerbations People with emphysema may experience acute exacerbations,
where symptoms worsen for days or weeks. These may be triggered by
respiratory infections, air pollution, or other inflammatory factors.
Severe exacerbations can lead to lung failure and require urgent
treatment.

When to Seek Emergency Care Go to the emergency department if: - You
cannot catch your breath or speak properly - Lips or fingernails turn
blue or gray - You are confused or not mentally alert

Causes Emphysema develops due to long-term exposure to airborne
irritants, including:

-   Cigarette smoking (most common cause)
-   Cigar, pipe, or marijuana smoke
-   Secondhand smoke
-   Chemical fumes
-   Workplace vapors and dust
-   Indoor and outdoor air pollution

Genetic Cause A rare inherited condition called alpha-1 antitrypsin
(AAT) deficiency can cause emphysema at a younger age. Low levels of AAT
reduce lung protection against damage.

Risk Factors - Smoking (current or past) - Exposure to secondhand
smoke - Occupational exposure to fumes, dust or chemicals - Air
pollution exposure - Genetic factors (AAT deficiency) - Age over 40
(most cases develop after this age)

Complications - Pulmonary hypertension (high blood pressure in lung
arteries) - Cor pulmonale (right-sided heart failure) - Increased risk
of heart disease - Large air spaces in lungs (bullae) - Collapsed lung
(pneumothorax) - Lung cancer - Anxiety and depression

Prevention - Do not smoke - Avoid secondhand smoke - Use protective
equipment if exposed to workplace fumes or dust - Reduce exposure to air
pollution

Prognosis Emphysema is progressive and irreversible. Early diagnosis and
smoking cessation significantly improve long-term outcomes and slow
disease progression.
