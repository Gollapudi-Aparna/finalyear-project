Pneumonia
Overview

Pneumonia is a lung infection that causes inflammation in the air sacs (alveoli) of one or both lungs. These air sacs may fill with fluid or pus, leading to symptoms such as cough with phlegm, fever, chills, and difficulty breathing.

The disease can be caused by bacteria, viruses, or fungi. Pneumonia can range from mild to life-threatening, especially in:

Infants and young children

Adults over 65 years

People with weakened immune systems or existing health conditions

Symptoms

Symptoms can range from mild to severe depending on the organism causing the infection, age, and overall health.

Common Symptoms

Chest pain while breathing or coughing

Persistent cough with phlegm

Fever, sweating, and chills

Shortness of breath

Fatigue

Nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea

Symptoms in Older Adults

Confusion

Lower than normal body temperature

Symptoms in Infants

Vomiting

Fever and cough

Difficulty breathing

Trouble feeding

Weakness or restlessness

When to See a Doctor

Medical attention is necessary if a person experiences:

Difficulty breathing

Chest pain

Persistent fever above 102°F (39°C)

Continuous cough with pus

People who must seek medical care quickly include:

Adults above 65 years

Children below 2 years

People with weak immune systems

Patients receiving chemotherapy

Causes of Pneumonia

Pneumonia occurs when germs enter the lungs and overcome the immune system.

Common causes include:

Bacteria

Viruses

Fungi

These germs usually spread through the air.

Types of Pneumonia
1. Community-Acquired Pneumonia

The most common type. It develops outside hospitals or healthcare facilities.

Causes include:

Bacteria:
The most common bacterial cause is Streptococcus pneumoniae. It may affect a single lobe of the lung (lobar pneumonia).

Mycoplasma pneumoniae:
Causes mild pneumonia often called walking pneumonia.

Fungi:
Occurs mostly in people with weak immune systems. The organisms are commonly found in soil or bird droppings.

Viruses:
Viruses that cause cold and flu can lead to pneumonia. COVID-19 can also cause severe pneumonia.

2. Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia

This type occurs during a hospital stay for another illness.
It can be serious because:

Patients are already ill

Bacteria may be resistant to antibiotics

People on ventilators are at higher risk.

3. Health-Care Associated Pneumonia

Occurs in patients receiving long-term medical care, such as:

Nursing homes

Dialysis centers

These infections may involve antibiotic-resistant bacteria.

4. Aspiration Pneumonia

Occurs when food, drink, saliva, or vomit enters the lungs.
Risk factors include:

Brain injury

Swallowing disorders

Excess alcohol or drug use

Risk Factors

Anyone can develop pneumonia, but higher risk occurs in:

Age

Children under 2 years

Adults above 65 years

Medical Conditions

Asthma

COPD

Heart disease

Lifestyle

Smoking

Medical Situations

Hospitalization (especially ICU)

Ventilator support

Weak immune system

Chemotherapy

HIV/AIDS

Organ transplant

Complications

If untreated or severe, pneumonia can lead to serious complications.

1. Bacteremia

Bacteria enter the bloodstream and spread infection to other organs.

2. Breathing Problems

Severe pneumonia may require oxygen support or ventilators.

3. Pleural Effusion

Fluid accumulation between lung layers that may require drainage.

4. Lung Abscess

A pus-filled cavity in the lung that may need antibiotics or surgical drainage.

Prevention

Several measures can help reduce the risk of pneumonia.

Vaccination

Vaccines are available to protect against:

Pneumococcal infections

Influenza

Child Vaccination

Children under 2 and those at high risk should receive pneumonia vaccines.

Hygiene

Wash hands regularly

Use alcohol-based sanitizers

Avoid Smoking

Smoking damages the lungs and increases infection risk.

Healthy Lifestyle

Proper sleep

Regular exercise

Balanced diet
