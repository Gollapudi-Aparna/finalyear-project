Inguinal Hernia

Overview An inguinal hernia occurs when tissue, such as part of the
intestine, protrudes through a weak spot in the abdominal muscles. The
resulting bulge may be painful, especially when coughing, bending over
or lifting heavy objects. Some hernias may not cause pain but do not
resolve on their own.

Surgical repair is commonly recommended, particularly if the hernia is
painful, enlarging, or causing complications.

Symptoms - Bulge on either side of the pubic bone (more noticeable when
standing, coughing or straining) - Burning or aching sensation at the
bulge - Pain, pressure or discomfort in the groin - Pain and swelling
around the testicles (in men, if intestine descends into scrotum)

Symptoms in Children - Visible bulge when crying, coughing or
straining - Irritability - Reduced appetite - More noticeable when
standing or straining

Signs of Complications

Incarcerated Hernia Occurs when hernia contents become trapped in the
abdominal wall. - Severe pain - Nausea or vomiting - Inability to pass
stool or gas

Strangulated Hernia (Medical Emergency) Occurs when blood supply to
trapped tissue is cut off. - Sudden severe pain - Fever - Nausea or
vomiting - Hernia bulge turns red, purple or dark - Bowel obstruction
symptoms

Seek immediate medical care if strangulation symptoms occur.

Causes - Increased pressure inside the abdomen - Preexisting weakness in
abdominal wall - Straining during bowel movements or urination - Heavy
lifting or prolonged standing - Pregnancy - Chronic coughing or
sneezing - Weakness present at birth - Aging-related muscle weakening -
Previous abdominal injury or surgery

In men, hernias commonly occur in the inguinal canal where the spermatic
cord passes. In women, hernias may occur near the ligament supporting
the uterus.

Risk Factors - Male gender (higher risk than women) - Older age - Family
history of hernia - Chronic cough (often from smoking) - Chronic
constipation - Pregnancy - Premature birth or low birth weight -
Connective tissue disorders (e.g., Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, Marfan
syndrome) - Previous hernia or hernia repair - History of prostate
surgery

Complications - Enlargement of hernia over time - Pressure on
surrounding tissues - Incarceration (trapped bowel) - Strangulation
(loss of blood supply) - Bowel obstruction - Tissue death (if untreated)

Prevention While congenital weakness cannot be prevented, risk can be
reduced by: - Maintaining a healthy weight - Eating high-fiber foods to
prevent constipation - Lifting heavy objects properly (bend knees, not
waist) - Avoiding heavy lifting when possible - Quitting smoking to
reduce chronic cough

Prognosis Inguinal hernias do not improve without surgery. Surgical
repair is generally safe and effective, especially when performed before
complications develop.
