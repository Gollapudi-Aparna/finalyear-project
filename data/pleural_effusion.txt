Disease Name: Pleural Effusion

Overview:
Pleural effusion is a buildup of excess fluid between the layers of the pleura around the lungs. The pleura are thin membranes that line the lungs and the inside of the chest cavity. Normally a small amount of fluid is present to help the lungs move smoothly during breathing. In pleural effusion, too much fluid collects in this space, making breathing more difficult.

Pleural effusion is sometimes called “water on the lungs.” Treatment focuses on removing the extra fluid and treating the condition that caused it. The condition may occur more than once depending on the underlying cause.

Types of Pleural Effusion:

Transudative Pleural Effusion:
This type contains protein‑poor fluid and usually occurs due to pressure changes in blood vessels.
Common causes include heart failure, cirrhosis and nephrotic syndrome.

Exudative Pleural Effusion:
This type contains protein‑rich fluid and occurs due to inflammation, infection or cancer.
Common causes include pneumonia, cancer and kidney disease.

Symptoms:
Chest pain, especially when coughing or taking deep breaths
Shortness of breath
Difficulty breathing unless sitting upright
Some people may have no symptoms and discover it during imaging tests.

Causes:

Common causes of transudative effusion:
Heart failure
Cirrhosis
Nephrotic syndrome

Common causes of exudative effusion:
Pneumonia
Cancer such as lung cancer, breast cancer or lymphoma
Kidney disease
Inflammatory diseases
Post heart surgery

Less common causes:
Tuberculosis
Autoimmune diseases
Chest trauma
Chylothorax
Rare infections
Asbestos exposure
Pancreatitis
Certain medications
Radiation therapy
Abdominal surgery

Risk Factors:
Underlying medical conditions
Smoking or tobacco use
Exposure to asbestos

Complications:
Infection that may form an abscess
Scarring around the lungs
Damage to lung tissue

Diagnosis:
Doctors review medical history and perform a physical examination. Tests may include:

Chest X‑ray
CT scan of the chest
Chest ultrasound
Thoracentesis to remove fluid for testing
Pleural fluid analysis
Thoracoscopy (minimally invasive surgery to view the pleura)

Treatment:

Medications:
Diuretics for heart failure related effusion
Antibiotics for infections
Chemotherapy or radiation for cancer
Other medicines depending on the underlying cause

Procedures:
Thoracentesis to drain fluid
Chest tube drainage
Pleural sclerosis using medication to prevent recurrence

Surgery:
Video‑assisted thoracoscopic surgery (VATS)
Thoracotomy for severe infections

Possible Treatment Complications:
Pulmonary edema
Blood clots
Abnormal heart rhythms
Collapsed lung (pneumothorax)

Recovery:
Recovery time depends on the procedure. Some patients recover within a few days, while major surgery may require a longer hospital stay.

Outlook:
The outcome depends on the underlying cause, how well treatment works and whether the fluid returns. Some cases resolve completely while others may recur.

Prevention:
Avoid tobacco products
Avoid asbestos exposure
Follow low‑salt diet if recommended
Take prescribed medications
Follow medical advice for underlying conditions

When to Seek Medical Care:
Contact a healthcare provider if breathing becomes difficult or symptoms worsen. Regular follow‑ups help monitor the condition.
